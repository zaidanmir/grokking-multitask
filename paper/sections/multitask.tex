\section{Multi-task experiments}
\label{sec:multitask}

\subsection{Two operations: add + subtract}

Figure \ref{fig:fig3_multitask_two} shows per-task test accuracy when a
single network is trained jointly on addition and subtraction. Both
operations live on the additive group of $\Z/p\Z$, so the natural
prediction is that the model can solve both with a shared additive
Fourier basis. We find that the two tasks grok in near-lockstep:
addition reaches the 0.95 grok threshold at step 5,800 and subtraction
at step 6,000. Notably this is \emph{earlier} than either single-task
baseline (7,200 and 7,000 respectively, Table
\ref{tab:grok_steps_single}). One possible explanation: the joint
gradient signal pulls the additive Fourier basis into existence faster
than either task could on its own, because both tasks contribute to
the same low-rank embedding sub-space.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{figures/fig3_multitask_two.pdf}
\caption{Multi-task training on $\textsc{add} + \textsc{sub}$. Per-task
test accuracy as a function of training step. Both tasks grok within
the same training budget, and faster than the single-task baselines.}
\label{fig:fig3_multitask_two}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Three operations: add + subtract + multiply}
\label{sec:mt3}

The full multi-task experiment trains the same architecture jointly on
all three operations. Figure \ref{fig:fig4_multitask_three} shows the
per-task dynamics across 75{,}000 training steps.

\paragraph{Slow-ramp dynamics, not a sudden grok.}
Unlike single-task and two-task experiments, the three-task model does
\emph{not} undergo the canonical grokking transition. Instead of a long
flat plateau at random-guess accuracy followed by an abrupt collapse
of test loss, the test accuracy on each task rises smoothly from
roughly $\log p \approx 4.7$ random-guess loss (test acc $\sim 0.01$)
down toward partial generalisation over a long ramp:
\begin{itemize}
\item Test accuracies at step 75{,}000 are
      \textsc{add}: $0.604$, \textsc{sub}: $0.483$, \textsc{mul}: $0.324$
      --- well above random ($1/p \approx 0.009$) but well below the
      $0.95$ grok threshold.
\item No task crosses 0.95 within the 75{,}000-step budget. By
      extrapolating the late-training slope, full grokking would
      require an estimated $200{,}000$+ steps.
\item The per-task ordering is consistent throughout: addition leads,
      subtraction follows, multiplication trails.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why is multi-task three so much slower?}
A natural explanation is that the residual stream must accommodate
\emph{two} algorithmically distinct mechanisms simultaneously --- an
additive-group Fourier circuit for $+$ and $-$, and a multiplicative-
group Fourier circuit for $\times$. The two circuits compete for
capacity, and the optimiser must balance them under aggressive weight
decay. The smooth ramp suggests a continuous reorganisation of the
representation rather than the abrupt phase transition seen when only
one circuit is needed.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/fig4_multitask_three.pdf}
\caption{Multi-task training on $\{+, -, \times\} \bmod 113$. Per-task
test accuracy as a function of training step (seed 42, 75{,}000 steps).
None of the tasks reach the 0.95 grok threshold within this budget;
the dynamics are a slow ramp rather than the abrupt transition of
the single- and two-task experiments.}
\label{fig:fig4_multitask_three}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Robustness across seeds and configurations}
\label{sec:robustness}

We re-ran the three-task experiment with a different random seed
(137) under the same step budget and confirmed the slow-ramp
qualitative dynamic. A second additional seed (271) and a curriculum
warm-start (training on addition to grokking, then introducing
subtraction and multiplication) are scheduled for follow-up runs on
a CUDA GPU and are not included in this draft.

For the single-task addition baseline we ran a quick robustness
sweep across the modulus $p$ and the train fraction. Table
\ref{tab:robustness} reports the grok step in each cell. The
qualitative grokking behaviour is robust across all cells; the grok
step shrinks as the train fraction grows.
