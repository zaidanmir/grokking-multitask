\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Grokking is the phenomenon in which a small neural network trained on a
synthetic algorithmic task first memorises the training set and then,
after thousands of additional training steps with no apparent progress,
abruptly generalises. \citet{nanda2023progress} showed that during the
apparent plateau a one-layer transformer is silently constructing a
discrete-Fourier-transform algorithm for modular addition. We extend that
analysis to a \emph{multi-task} setting in which a single one-layer
transformer is trained jointly on three modular arithmetic tasks ---
addition, subtraction, and multiplication modulo a prime $p$. Two of these
tasks live on the additive group $\Z/p\Z$ and one lives on the
multiplicative group $(\Z/p\Z)^*$, which has different Fourier-transform
structure. We replicate the single-task addition baseline at the published
hyperparameters, characterise per-task grokking dynamics in the joint
setting, and analyse the trained embedding matrix to ask whether the
discovered Fourier features are shared across tasks or task-specific.
We find that addition and subtraction grok jointly with a shared additive
basis, while multiplication develops a separate, multiplicative-group basis
in the same residual stream. Per-head ablations confirm that attention
heads specialise toward operation-conditional features. Code, configs, and
all per-step metric histories are open-sourced for full reproducibility.
\end{abstract}
