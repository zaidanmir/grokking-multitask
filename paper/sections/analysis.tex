\section{Mechanistic analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Fourier features in single-task models}

We extract the digit sub-matrix $W_E[0:p, :] \in \R^{p \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$
of the trained embedding, take the DFT along the digit axis as in equation
\eqref{eq:dft}, and plot the per-frequency power spectrum in Figure
\ref{fig:fig6_fourier_singletask}.

The addition and subtraction models concentrate power on a small set of
\emph{additive-group} frequencies. The addition model has four dominant
frequencies $\{17, 26, 35, 39\}$ (and their conjugates $\{p - k\}$),
matching the small-$K$ structure predicted by \citet{nanda2023progress}.
Subtraction also has a sparse additive-group spectrum but at
\emph{different} frequencies $\{22, 30, 40\}$. The multiplication model
has a much flatter additive-group spectrum --- the Fourier features it
has learned do not live on the additive cyclic group of order $p$.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/fig6_fourier_singletask.pdf}
\caption{Additive-group Fourier power spectrum of the digit embedding
$W_E[0:p, :]$ for the three single-task models. Top: addition. Middle:
subtraction. Bottom: multiplication. Power is concentrated on a small
set of frequencies for the additive tasks (different frequencies
between add and sub); multiplication's spectrum on this basis is
essentially flat.}
\label{fig:fig6_fourier_singletask}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The multiplicative-group basis}

Reordering the digit embeddings by powers of a primitive root of $p$
and taking the DFT on $(\Z/p\Z)^*$ yields the multiplicative-group
spectrum. Figure \ref{fig:fig7_fourier_mult} compares the two bases
on the multiplication model's embedding: the additive basis is
essentially flat, while the multiplicative basis exhibits sparse
spike structure at frequencies $\{32, 33, 49, 50, \ldots\}$. This is
direct evidence that the network has discovered the multiplicative
analogue of the Fourier algorithm.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/fig7_fourier_mult.pdf}
\caption{Multiplicative-group Fourier power spectrum of the digit
embedding for the multiplication model. Same embedding matrix in
both panels. \emph{Top:} additive basis $\Z/p\Z$ --- flat, no
structure. \emph{Bottom:} multiplicative basis $(\Z/p\Z)^*$ ---
sparse spikes at a small set of frequencies, mirroring the additive-
group structure of the addition model.}
\label{fig:fig7_fourier_mult}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Multi-task model: which features does it use?}

The headline question is whether the multi-task model uses a single
shared feature set or separate task-specific bases. At 75{,}000 steps
the multi-task model has only partially generalised (Section
\ref{sec:mt3}), so its representation is mid-development rather than
in a clean steady state.

Figure \ref{fig:fig8_feature_overlap} shows the cosine similarity
between the per-frequency power spectra of the multi-task model and
each single-task model, both on the additive and the multiplicative
bases. Two observations:
\begin{itemize}
\item On the multiplicative basis, the multi-task model overlaps
      \emph{more} with the addition and subtraction single-task models
      ($\sim 0.6$) than with the multiplication single-task model
      ($\sim 0.14$).
\item On the additive basis, the multi-task model overlaps
      \emph{most} with the multiplication single-task model
      ($\sim 0.57$) and \emph{least} with the addition single-task
      model ($\sim 0.05$).
\end{itemize}
Both observations follow from the diffuseness of the multi-task
model's spectra at this training horizon: the cosine similarity is
high between two spread-out vectors and low between a spread-out vector
and a sparse one. Concretely, the multi-task model has not yet
concentrated its additive-basis power onto a small set of frequencies
(the way the single-task addition model has), and similarly for the
multiplicative basis. We expect this picture to clarify with longer
training; the cleanest predictions of the analysis (multi-task model
shares the dominant-frequency set with the additive single-task models
on the additive basis, and with the single-task multiplication model
on the multiplicative basis) require a fully-grokked multi-task
checkpoint that we do not yet have.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/fig8_feature_overlap.pdf}
\caption{Cosine similarity between Fourier power spectra of the
multi-task model and each single-task model, on the additive
(\emph{left bars}) and the multiplicative (\emph{right bars}) basis.
The spectra are taken at the partially-grokked checkpoint (test
accuracy $\sim 0.5$); the multi-task model has not yet committed to
either basis sharply, which is reflected in the overlap pattern.}
\label{fig:fig8_feature_overlap}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Per-head ablations on the multi-task model}

Figure \ref{fig:fig9_head_ablation} shows the per-task loss increase
when each of the four attention heads in the multi-task model is
ablated in turn. Three findings:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Multiplication is more sensitive to ablation of any single head
      than addition or subtraction: every head's removal produces a
      larger loss increase on \textsc{mul} (5.77--6.97) than on the
      additive tasks (2.5--5.4).
\item Head 0 is the most critical for addition and subtraction
      (loss increases of 5.41 and 5.33 respectively, vs.\ $\sim 2.6$
      for the other heads).
\item Heads 0 and 3 are jointly critical for multiplication.
\end{enumerate}
The pattern is consistent with partial specialisation: head 0 is a
``primarily additive'' head that all three tasks depend on, while
heads 1 and 2 are nearly redundant for the additive tasks but still
contribute to multiplication. None of the heads is task-exclusive ---
ablating any single head degrades all three tasks --- but the
contributions are quantitatively asymmetric.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{figures/fig9_head_ablation.pdf}
\caption{Per-head, per-task loss increase under attention-head
ablation on the multi-task model (test set). Brighter cells indicate
that ablating the head causes a larger loss increase on the
corresponding operation. Multiplication is uniformly harder hit.}
\label{fig:fig9_head_ablation}
\end{figure}
